## Why Haiti Is Different

Every other country's termination went through. Haiti's was blocked at the last minute — February 2, 2026, one day before it would have taken effect — by a federal judge in *Miot v. Trump*.

The question now: **for how long?**

E-Verify's July 10, 2026 alert calls the stay "limited relief until the lower courts align with the Supreme Court's decision." The Supreme Court ruled in *Mullin* on June 25, 2026 in a way DHS considers favorable. Once the D.C. district court reviews that ruling, the stay could lift.

## The Pattern Is Clear

DHS under Noem has systematically ended TPS across the board. The rationale is consistent: the Secretary reviews country conditions, consults with agencies, and determines conditions no longer warrant TPS. Haiti was treated no differently — it just had a court willing to intervene.

This is not random. This is policy.

## What This Means for Advocacy

Haitian TPS holders cannot count on administrative relief from DHS. The only durable solutions are:

1. **Legislation** — HR1689 (mandatory Haiti TPS designation) and HR9494 (path to permanent residence)
2. **Courts** — The *Miot* stay, while temporary, buys time for legislative action
3. **Individual planning** — Every Haitian TPS holder should explore other immigration options (asylum, family petitions, employment-based)

## The Clock Is Ticking

If the Miot stay lifts, Haiti TPS ends. If HR1689 passes, it doesn't matter — the law would require re-designation. But if neither happens, Haitian TPS holders face the same fate as every other country on the list above.
