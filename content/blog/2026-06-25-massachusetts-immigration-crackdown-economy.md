# The Math Doesn't Work: How the Immigration Crackdown Is Quietly Breaking Massachusetts' Economy

**Read time:** ~6 min

**Excerpt:** There's a number that should worry every business owner, hospital administrator, and homeowner in Massachusetts: 60,000 — the immigrants the state needs every year just to keep its workforce from shrinking. It's not happening, and a Supreme Court ruling this week just made it worse.

---

There's a number that should worry every business owner, hospital administrator, and homeowner in Massachusetts: **60,000**. That's how many new immigrants the state needs *every single year* between now and 2030 just to keep its working-age population from shrinking. Not growing — just standing still.

It's not happening. Net international migration to Massachusetts fell by more than half in the first six months of the second Trump administration, according to ["An Uncertain Future: How the Immigration Crackdown Threatens Massachusetts' Labor Force,"](https://www.bostonindicators.org/reports/report-detail-pages/2026/june/immigration-workforce) a June 2026 report from Boston Indicators and the MassINC Policy Center. And on June 25, 2026 — the same week this story is being written — the U.S. Supreme Court handed down a ruling that could accelerate the bleeding.

This isn't an abstract policy debate. It's a math problem with a closing window, and Massachusetts is running out of the one input it can't replace fast enough: people.

## A State That Needs Immigrants to Stand Still

Massachusetts has the second-lowest native-born birth rate in the country, and people keep leaving for cheaper states. For two decades, immigration has been the only thing keeping the state's population — and its workforce — from contracting outright. Immigrants now make up roughly a quarter of the state's labor force while accounting for about a fifth of its population, according to the Boston Indicators report, a finding echoed by [migration and labor data](https://usafacts.org/answers/what-percent-of-jobs-in-the-us-are-held-by-immigrants/state/massachusetts/) showing immigrant labor force participation in Massachusetts (71.2%) running well ahead of the native-born rate (66.0%).

The dollars attached to that labor are not small. Immigrant households in Massachusetts commanded an estimated **$50.5 billion in spending power** in 2024, while paying roughly **$7.4 billion in state and local taxes** and more than **$23 billion in federal taxes**. Pull that population out quickly, and you're not just losing workers — you're draining billions in economic activity from a state that is already grappling with high housing costs, an aging population, and slowing growth.

## Three Industries Already Feeling It

The labor force impact isn't evenly spread. It follows what researchers describe as a "U-shaped" pattern — concentrated at both ends of the skill spectrum, in elite research roles and in the blue-collar and service jobs that keep daily life running.

**Healthcare and senior care.** Immigrants make up about a quarter of the state's healthcare and social-assistance workforce. Massachusetts nursing facilities employ thousands of Haitian workers — licensed nurses, certified nursing assistants, food service and laundry staff — inside facilities that already report direct-care vacancy rates near 13%, even before accounting for immigration losses. The Massachusetts Senior Care Association has warned that losing Temporary Protected Status (TPS) workers could force facilities to cap new admissions, which backs patients up in hospitals and worsens ER bottlenecks. Fear of ICE is compounding the problem on the patient side too: immigrants are reportedly skipping or delaying their own medical care.

**Higher education and the innovation economy.** International students are a quiet pillar of the state's research engine. Massachusetts lost over 5,000 international students between March 2025 and March 2026 — the second-largest drop of any state — and researchers estimate a 30% decline in foreign enrollment could cost the state more than **$1.4 billion a year**. Add to that a new $100,000 federal fee on H-1B visa applications, which is brutal for early-stage biotech and life-science startups that can't absorb that cost per hire, and you get a quieter, harder-to-see form of attrition: more than 40% of Massachusetts scientists surveyed say researchers in their labs have already relocated abroad over the past year.

**Construction and housing.** Immigrants make up roughly 30% of the state's construction workforce, and contractors report that even *rumors* of an ICE raid can empty a job site for the day. Replacing a crew mid-project doesn't just cost money — every delay compounds against high interest rates and rising material costs, at the exact moment Massachusetts is trying to build its way out of a housing shortage.

## The Cities That Can't Absorb the Shock

Zoom into the state's Gateway Cities — Everett, Malden, Revere, Lawrence, Chelsea, and two dozen others — and the stakes sharpen further. These former industrial hubs hold about a quarter of the state's population but nearly 40% of its foreign-born residents, with cities like Chelsea (45% foreign-born), Malden (43%), and Everett (43%) among the most immigrant-dense communities in New England. These are also the places where the next generation of the state's labor force is already being raised: in many Gateway Cities, children of immigrant parents will make up a third or more of all new labor-force entrants over the next decade. A sudden contraction doesn't just shrink a workforce — it destabilizes the exact communities the state is counting on to rebuild it.

## A Climate of Fear, by Design

The economic story is inseparable from the human one. "Operation Patriot" and its sequel swept through Massachusetts in 2025, with federal agents arresting nearly 3,000 people combined. ICE and DHS have framed the operations around violent offenders and gang members with outstanding warrants — and some of those arrested did have serious criminal records. But the dragnet reached well beyond that framing, and its chilling effect has been broad and indiscriminate, hitting workplaces, job sites, and households that had nothing to do with any of it.

The case that became a national flashpoint happened in Somerville: Tufts PhD student Rümeysa Öztürk was pulled off the street by plainclothes agents in March 2025 after co-authoring a student newspaper op-ed — not over any criminal allegation. She spent weeks in detention before a federal judge ordered her release. She finished her PhD in February 2026, settled her case with the government that April, and went home to Turkey. She was here legally, on a valid visa, the entire time agents put her in handcuffs.

## Then, This Week, the Floor Dropped Further

On June 25, 2026, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled 6–3 that the Trump administration can proceed with ending TPS for Haitian and Syrian nationals, holding that the courts have essentially no authority to second-guess how the executive branch makes that call. The ruling touches roughly 330,000 Haitians and thousands of Syrians living and working legally across the country — many of them in Massachusetts, many of them inside the same nursing homes and hospitals already short-staffed.

Governor Maura Healey didn't soften her response: *"Donald Trump is ripping away protections from families and children who have fled devastating earthquakes, violence and other humanitarian crises to build safe, productive lives here in Massachusetts... These are people who are raising families, paying taxes, caring for our loved ones, opening businesses and filling critical jobs."* She added that while the state can't control federal policy, "we can control how Massachusetts shows up" — pointing to new state resources for legal aid and a partnership with United Way to help affected families with housing, food, and child care.

Boston Mayor Michelle Wu has leaned on a simpler message, delivered in multiple languages over the past year: *"You belong here. This is your home."*

## The Bottom Line

Strip away the politics for a second, and what's left is an accounting problem. Massachusetts needs 60,000 net new immigrants a year just to hold its workforce steady — and instead it's losing students, scientists, construction crews, and caregivers, all at once, to a mix of policy terminations, sky-high visa fees, and plain fear. The people leaving aren't an abstraction in a federal press release. They're the CNA on the night shift, the framer on the job site, the lab tech keeping a biotech trial on schedule. Pull enough of them out at once, and it isn't just a humanitarian story anymore — it's a state running short on the one resource it can't print more of.
