# Springfield Spent a Decade Rebuilding Around Haitian Immigrants. The Supreme Court Just Undid It.

**Read time:** ~3 min

**Excerpt:** A Supreme Court ruling just stripped legal status from more than 10,000 Haitians in Ohio — most of them in Springfield, the city they helped pull out of a 50-year decline. Their driver's licenses expire July 6. What they lose after that is still being decided in church basements and legal aid offices across the city.

---

## A Ruling That Reaches Deep Into Ohio

On June 25, 2026, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled 6–3 that the Trump administration can end Temporary Protected Status (TPS) for Haitians and Syrians. The decision strips legal status from more than **330,000 Haitians** nationwide — and it lands hardest in one Ohio city: Springfield.

Springfield is home to an estimated **10,000 to 15,000 Haitian immigrants**, though some counts run as high as 20,000. No one has an exact figure, because residents hold different forms of legal status. What is clear: more than **10,000 Haitians** across Ohio, most of them in the Springfield area, have been living and working here legally through TPS. After this ruling, that status disappears, and so does their right to work.

The clock is already running. Affected residents will lose their valid Ohio driver's licenses on **July 6**, according to Advocates for Basic Legal Equality (ABLE), the legal aid group representing Haitian TPS holders in Ohio. Losing a license means losing the ability to drive to work, to school, or to church, in a city with limited public transit.

## The City That Came Back

Springfield's relationship with its Haitian community is not new, and it is not simple. For decades, Springfield was one of the fastest-shrinking cities in America. Then Haitian immigrants began arriving for manufacturing and warehouse jobs, and the population trend reversed.

Pastor Carl Ruby, a Springfield faith leader who has worked closely with the Haitian community, put it plainly after the ruling: **"We went from being the fastest shrinking city in America to the fastest growing. And this undoes all of that progress."** Ruby called the decision "the worst possible outcome" and said, **"I love my country, but I'm ashamed of it right now."**

Republican Gov. Mike DeWine — not a typical ally on immigration politics — made a similar point from across the aisle. DeWine called the ruling **"a mistake,"** arguing that Haitians who were legally working and contributing to Springfield's economy one day became unemployable the next, simply because of a court decision.

## "Sending Them Back Is Like Sentencing Them to Death"

For Haitian families in Springfield, the ruling is not abstract. Haiti remains gripped by gang violence, political collapse, and food insecurity, and aid groups have repeatedly warned that deportations are sending people back into danger.

Viles Dorsainvil, executive director of Springfield's Haitian Support Center, said it most directly: **"Sending Haitians back to Haiti is like sentencing them to death."** Community organizers who work with Haitian families say many would gladly return home if Haiti were safe. For now, TPS has been the only thing standing between them and that danger.

Some Springfield faith leaders are now discussing whether to formally designate their churches as sanctuary spaces for immigrants facing removal.

## A Hoax That Never Really Left

This ruling does not land in a vacuum. In September 2024, false claims that Haitian immigrants in Springfield were stealing and eating residents' pets spread rapidly online, amplified by then-vice-presidential candidate JD Vance and Donald Trump. Springfield police, the city's Republican mayor, and Gov. DeWine all said the claims had no factual basis.

The hoax triggered bomb threats against schools and government buildings and was later named PolitiFact's "Lie of the Year" for 2024. The rumor faded from headlines, but advocates say the climate of suspicion it created never fully lifted. This ruling lands inside that same climate.

## What Happens Next

Without intervention, Ohio's Haitian TPS holders move from legal resident to deportation target within days, not months. Advocates are pushing on multiple fronts at once: legal challenges to slow individual cases, sanctuary designations at the local level, and public pressure on state and federal officials.

Springfield has been here before, in a sense — rebuilt by people much of the country was told to fear. What happens next depends on whether that rebuilding gets to continue.
