Six months into 2026, Haiti TPS exists in a strange state: terminated by DHS, saved by a court, challenged at the Supreme Court, and debated in Congress. Here's where everything stands right now, in plain language.

## What's Protecting You Right Now

**The Miot v. Trump court stay.** That's it. One federal judge in D.C. blocked the February 3, 2026 termination the day before it took effect. Without that order, Haiti TPS would be gone — like Nicaragua, Honduras, Nepal, Cameroon, Ethiopia, and a dozen other countries whose TPS Secretary Noem has already terminated.

## What Changed in June-July 2026

Three major developments:

### 1. The Supreme Court Weighed In (June 25)

The Court issued rulings in *Mullin v. Doe* and *Mullin v. Al Otro Lado*. DHS calls these rulings favorable to its TPS termination authority. The D.C. district court now needs to review them — and could lift the Miot stay at any time.

### 2. E-Verify Issued I-9 Guidance (July 10)

For the first time, employers have clear instructions for Haitian TPS holders: enter "as per court order" in Section 1 and "July 24, 2026" in Section 2. The guidance also confirms the stay is "limited relief."

### 3. Congress Introduced HR9494 (June 25)

Rep. Frederica Wilson's Haitian Refugee Immigration Fairness Act would create a direct path to green cards for qualifying Haitian nationals. It's early — referred to Judiciary Committee — but it's the most ambitious Haiti immigration bill of this Congress.

## What Didn't Change

- **Haiti TPS is still in effect.** The USCIS page confirms it.
- **EADs are still auto-extended.** The list of eligible expiration dates hasn't changed.
- **HR1689 is still on the Senate Calendar.** It passed the House with bipartisan support but awaits Senate action.
- **DHS still wants to terminate.** The underlying policy position hasn't shifted.
